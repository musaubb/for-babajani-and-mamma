A Letter I Should Have
Written Long Ago

Dear Babajani & Mamma,

There are things a son carries quietly — gratitude so heavy it sits in his chest like a second heartbeat. I have been carrying mine across 4,000 miles, through two countries, through a failure that should have ended everything — and I never once put it into words. Until now.

You didn't just send me to the UK. You sent me your trust — wrapped in every tuition payment, folded into every rent transfer, hidden inside every phone call where you said "don't worry about the money." But I know what that money costs. I know the weight of it. And I know you carried it so I wouldn't have to.

When I failed my first year, I couldn't sleep for days. I stared at my phone, rehearsing apologies that would never be enough. I thought you'd say come home. I thought the dream was over.

But you said "try again."
Two words. And they rebuilt me.

Most parents would have called it enough. Most parents would have been right to. But you — you chose me again. Not because I earned it. Because you loved me more than my mistakes.

I live in the city centre of Birmingham. I know what that costs you. I know you chose my comfort over your own. Every lecture I sit in, every morning I wake up here — that is not my life. That is yours, given to me.

So this is not just a thank you. This is a son telling his parents: I see you. I see every sacrifice you thought I didn't notice. I see it all. And I am going to spend the rest of my life making sure it was worth it.

I will make you proud.
That is not a hope — it is a promise.

With all my love, always,

Your Son
